The problem
Adult researchers consistently misinterpret youth-oriented social media content. When designing studies on how youth interact with nicotine content online, the research team faced a fundamental gap: the people running the research weren't the people being studied. Standard approaches, such as adult-designed surveys, and adult-led coding, were easily missing what mattered to the actual users.

Research questions

1. How can user language inform better research instruments?

2. How do different user groups make sense of the same social media content?

3. How can participatory methods reduce researcher assumptions in youth-centered research?

Selected Case Studies in Youth-Centered Research
Case Study 1 participant experience evaluation for a youth collaborative program
1
Defined the participant experience problem
Evaluated whether youth participants experienced the MYTH Youth Collaborative as meaningful, inclusive, and worth continuing, rather than assuming that participation itself was automatically empowering.
2
Collected feedback across channels
Used evaluation surveys and focus group discussions to capture both structured feedback and deeper reflections on what participants valued, what felt engaging, and what could be improved.
3
Translated participant feedback into experience improvements
Synthesized survey and focus group findings into actionable recommendations for improving the collaborative experience, including more frequent meetings to strengthen learning, engagement, and continuity.
"Meaningful participation requires more than inviting youth into the room. It requires understanding whether the experience feels valuable, reciprocal, and worth continuing from their perspective."
Case Study 2 understanding content interpretation across user groups
1
Compared interpretations across user groups
Brought together three coder groups, including high school students, undergraduates, and faculty through a vertically intergrated project (VIP) approach to analyze the same 300 YouTube Shorts in parallel and examine how different backgrounds shaped content interpretation.
2
Tracked where interpretation aligned or diverged
Ran six iterative coding rounds, with each team double-coding 50 videos per round. Used inter-rater reliability metrics to identify which content categories were consistently interpreted and which remained ambiguous.
3
Facilitated a moderated research debrief
Led a 90-minute Zoom debrief with all coders using a semi-structured guide to understand why teams disagreed, how they resolved ambiguity, and what their disagreements revealed about youth-oriented content.
4
Turned disagreement into insight
Synthesized reliability patterns and debrief themes to show that disagreement was not just coding error. It revealed how age, cultural background, prior research experience, and lived experience shape how people make sense of the same content.
"When different groups interpreted the same content differently, the disagreement became the insight."

Case Study 2 Finding — disagreement became a source of insight

The VIP study showed that differences across high school, undergraduate, and faculty coding teams were not simply coding problems. They revealed how age, cultural background, prior research experience, and lived experience shaped how people interpreted the same youth-oriented social media content.

Coders found that some constructs were difficult to apply consistently because short-form videos often relied on humor, aesthetics, platform norms, and implied meaning. The debrief helped explain where the codebook worked, where it broke down, and how future research protocols could be improved.
